2. Matrix A times every column of B

[⋮⋮⋮⋮⋮⋮⋮⋮⋮⋮⋮⋮⋮⋮⋮⋮][B1B2⋯Bp]=[C1C2⋯Cp](A)(columns of B)=(columns of C)(1.2)\begin{aligned} \begin{bmatrix} \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ \end{bmatrix} & \begin{bmatrix} B_1 & B_2 & \cdots & B_p \end{bmatrix} & = & \begin{bmatrix} C_1 & C_2 & \cdots & C_p \end{bmatrix} \\[8ex] (A)\hspace{2em} & \hspace{0.8em}(columns\ of\ B) & = & \hspace{.7em} (columns\ of\ C) \end{aligned} \tag{1.2} ⎣⎢⎢⎢⎢⎢⎢⎡​⋮⋮⋮⋮​⋮⋮⋮⋮​⋮⋮⋮⋮​⋮⋮⋮⋮​⎦⎥⎥⎥⎥⎥⎥⎤​(A)​[B1​​B2​​⋯​Bp​​](columns of B)​==​[C1​​C2​​⋯​Cp​​](columns of C)​(1.2)

As mentioned in last post[1], a matrix multiplies a vector means a combination of columns in the matrix. Thus, every column of CCC has the same length of evey column of AAA, i.e., mmm. So, CCC is a m×pm\times pm×p matrix.

3. Every row of A times matrix B

[A1A2A3⋮Am][⋯⋯⋯⋯⋯⋯⋯⋯⋯⋯⋯⋯⋯⋯⋯⋯]=[C1C2C3⋮Cm](rows of A)(B)=(rows of C)(1.3)\begin{aligned} \begin{bmatrix} A_1 \\[.6ex] A_2 \\[.6ex] A_3 \\[.6ex] \vdots \\[.6ex] A_m \end{bmatrix} & \begin{bmatrix} \cdots & \cdots & \cdots & \cdots \\ \cdots & \cdots & \cdots & \cdots \\ \cdots & \cdots & \cdots & \cdots \\ \cdots & \cdots & \cdots & \cdots \\ \end{bmatrix} & = & \begin{bmatrix} C_1 \\[.6ex] C_2 \\[.6ex] C_3 \\[.6ex] \vdots \\[.6ex] C_m \end{bmatrix} \\[8ex] (rows\ of\ A) & \hspace{4em}(B) & = & \hspace{.5em} (rows\ of\ C) \end{aligned} \tag{1.3} ⎣⎢⎢⎢⎢⎢⎢⎡​A1​A2​A3​⋮Am​​⎦⎥⎥⎥⎥⎥⎥⎤​(rows of A)​⎣⎢⎢⎡​⋯⋯⋯⋯​⋯⋯⋯⋯​⋯⋯⋯⋯​⋯⋯⋯⋯​⎦⎥⎥⎤​(B)​==​⎣⎢⎢⎢⎢⎢⎢⎡​C1​C2​C3​⋮Cm​​⎦⎥⎥⎥⎥⎥⎥⎤​(rows of C)​(1.3)

Multiply a row vector times a matrix equals a combination of rows in the matrix. Every row in CCC has the same length of every row in BBB, i.e., ppp.

4. Column 1 to n of A times rows 1 to n of B

[A1A2A3⋯An][B1B2B3⋮Bn]= A1B1+A2B2+A3B3+⋯+AnBn(columns of A)(rows of B)=(C)(1.4)\begin{aligned} \begin{bmatrix} A_1 & A_2 & A_3 & \cdots & A_n \end{bmatrix} & \begin{bmatrix} B_1 \\[.6ex] B_2 \\[.6ex] B_3 \\[.6ex] \vdots \\[.6ex] B_n \end{bmatrix} & = & \ A_1B_1 + A_2B_2 + A_3B_3 + \cdots + A_nB_n \\[8ex] (columns\ of\ A)\hspace{2em} & (rows\ of\ B) & = & \hspace{.5em} (C) \end{aligned} \tag{1.4} [A1​​A2​​A3​​⋯​An​​](columns of A)​⎣⎢⎢⎢⎢⎢⎢⎡​B1​B2​B3​⋮Bn​​⎦⎥⎥⎥⎥⎥⎥⎤​(rows of B)​==​ A1​B1​+A2​B2​+A3​B3​+⋯+An​Bn​(C)​(1.4)

AiA_iAi​ is a column vector with length mmm and BiB_iBi​ is a row vector with length ppp. So, AiBiA_iB_iAi​Bi​ is a (m×p)(m\times p)(m×p) matrix. In this way, C is actually a sum of nnn matrices with the order (m×p)(m\times p)(m×p).

Given a row vector RRR, a column vector CCC. MMM is the result of multiplying RRR times CCC. It's easy to find these:

  • every column of MMM is a multiply of RRR
  • every row of MMM is a multiply of CCC

So, in 1.4, CCC can also be interpreted as combinations of columns in A or rows in B.

Inverses (Square Matrices)

Whatever a matrix does, its inverse undoes it.

If a square matrix A has an inverse, then both A−1A=IA^{-1}A = IA−1A=I and AA−1=IAA^{-1} = IAA−1=I. A is invertible, or non-singular.

Ways to check if a matrix is invertible

Given a n×nn\times nn×n matrix.

Elimination

An invertible matrix can get nnn pivots after elimination (row exchange is not allowed).
